The answer depends upon your application design. After all, synchronization is an opt-in concept - any thread could choose to ignore locks and simply access shared objects regardless.

Similarly with your example, if you are sure the only access to hashMap2 is within the synchronized block shown in your example, then you are effectively protecting it with a lock, albeit a lock on another object.

It may be clearer for future maintainers to take locks on both objects before operating on them.
